How to use & what the numbers mean

Quick start

  1. Type your question into "What are you estimating?"
  2. Add a row for each factor in your reasoning chain. Each row contributes via × (multiply) or ÷ (divide).
  3. For each row, give a lower, best, and upper value.
  4. Read off the summary: a best guess (median) and a 90% range.

What is a Fermi estimate?

A Fermi estimate (named for physicist Enrico Fermi) breaks a hard "how many?" question into a chain of simpler factors you can guess at. Multiply those guesses together and you usually land within an order of magnitude of the truth — even when each individual guess could be off by 2–3×. The lower and upper values on each row encode your uncertainty about that factor; this tool propagates the uncertainty through 10,000 Monte Carlo trials so the final range honestly reflects compound doubt.

Multiply (×) vs divide (÷)

Most factors multiply into the running estimate. Some divide — typically a "per-X" rate when you're solving for X. Example: to estimate piano tuners in Chicago, multiply population × households/person × pianos/household × tunings/year. But the last factor (tunings per tuner per year) must divide, because tuners × tunings/tuner = total tunings, not the other way around. Click the × pill on any row to flip it to ÷.

Reading the result

  • Best guess is the median: half the trials came out below, half above.
  • Low and High are the 5th and 95th percentile — there is a 90% chance the true answer is between them. The remaining 10% is split as tail risk on either side.
  • The shaded band below the numbers shows the full distribution. Log scale (default) makes a log-normal look symmetric; switch to Linear if you want to see the underlying right-skew.

Input formats

Values accept plain numbers (2700000), scaled (2.7M, 300K, 1.2B), scientific (1e6), fractions (1/150), and percentages (33%). Commas are ignored, so 2,700,000 also works.

Keyboard shortcuts

  • Enter on the Upper input — add a new row below (or jump to the next row if one exists).
  • Ctrl + D (or ⌘ + D) when focused inside a row — duplicate that row.
  • Tab — move through inputs left-to-right, top-to-bottom.
